EEG Source Derived Coupling Between rAI and dACC in Patch Foraging Task

Investigating neural mechanisms underlying decision-making processes using advanced EEG analysis

Abstract

Overview of the research objectives and findings

Understanding the neural mechanisms underlying decision-making processes is critical in cognitive neuroscience. This study investigates the coupling between the right anterior insula (rAI) and dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) during different decisions in a patch-foraging task using electroencephalography (EEG) data.

The project aims to elucidate the dynamic interactions within the salience network, which is crucial for adaptive behaviour and decision-making. Using pre-collected EEG data from participants engaged in a patch-foraging task, we employed advanced analysis techniques including source localisation with Recursive Sparse Bayesian Learning (RSBL) and Transfer Entropy analysis.

The results revealed significant connectivity patterns, with variations observed between 'stay' and 'leave' decisions, indicating the differential engagement of the salience network during decision-making. These findings contribute to our understanding of the neural substrates of decision-making and highlight the importance of the rAI-dACC coupling in adaptive behaviour.

Methodology

Advanced techniques employed in the analysis

📊

Data Collection

32-channel EEG recordings at 500 Hz sampling rate during patch-foraging task with 5 participants

🧹

Preprocessing

EEGLAB-based pipeline including filtering (0.5-49 Hz), re-referencing with FCz, and artifact rejection using Cleanline

🧠

Source Localisation

Recursive Sparse Bayesian Learning (RSBL) algorithm to identify brain regions of interest

📈

Transfer Entropy

Kraskov's method for measuring directional information flow between rAI and dACC

🔄

Cross-Correlation

Temporal relationship analysis between brain regions using MATLAB's xcorr function

⏱️

Epoching

Segmentation of data from -1000ms to 0ms relative to decision onset for stay/leave events

Key Results

Significant findings from the analysis

Environment 1: Short Travel Times

Transfer entropy analysis revealed progressive strengthening of information flow between rAI and dACC:

Key Finding: Consistent increase from Stay 1 to Stay 5, with significant drop during leave decisions, suggesting reduced coordination when transitioning between patches.

Environment 2: Long Travel Times

Higher variability in transfer entropy patterns reflecting increased cognitive load:

Key Finding: Notable increases at Stay 4 and Stay 5, indicating heightened cognitive engagement in more challenging environments.

dACC Activity Patterns

Fluctuations in dACC activity aligned with critical decision points:

Key Finding: Peak activity reflected moments of maximum cognitive control and attention during the foraging task.
